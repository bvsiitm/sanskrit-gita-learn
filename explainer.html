The Problem

Sanskrit is not hard.
The way it is taught is.

Most students encounter Sanskrit in one of three ways: rote-learning of shlokas in school with no understanding, an intimidating grammar textbook with thousands of rules before a single sentence, or a translation they can read but cannot parse.

The Bhagavad Gita sits on millions of shelves. But reading it in Sanskrit — really reading it, knowing what each word is doing — remains out of reach for nearly everyone who wants to.

The Gita teaches Sanskrit grammar simply by existing. Every verse is a worked example. The question is how to use it.

✦
The Insight

The top 192 dhātus unlock nearly everything

A corpus analysis of Classical Sanskrit reveals that just 192 verbal roots account for the vast majority of verb usage across the Gita, Upanishads, and Mahābhārata. More strikingly: 95% of the dhātus needed to read the Bhagavad Gita are in this top-192 list.

This is the Sanskrit equivalent of learning the 1,000 most common English words to unlock 85% of spoken conversation. Frequency-first learning is not a shortcut — it is the most direct path.

The Method

Known → +1 → Drill → +1 → Drill → SRS

The learning sequence is built on one principle: always start from what the student already knows, then add exactly one new idea. Drill on that. Then add one more. After a few ideas, spaced repetition. The exit condition at every stage is mastery, not time spent.

0

Anchor — What you already know

Character names you recognise from the Mahābhārata. Arjuna, Krishna, Dhritarashtra. Now see them in Devanāgarī. Zero grammar load. Pure recognition. Confidence builder.

1

+1 · Vibhakti — The nominative ending

Sanskrit encodes grammatical role in the word itself — not word order. The subject takes Prathamā Vibhakti. Masculine singular = -ः. One rule. Every subject in the Gita.

2

+1 · Lakāra — The verb tense

Sanskrit modifies the root itself for tense — no auxiliary words. उवाच = Liṭ Lakāra, perfect tense, third person singular. He said. Already done. Complete.

3

+1 · Dhātu — The root

Every verb descends from a dhātu. उवाच comes from √वच् — to speak. Know the root, and nine other forms across the Gita become instantly recognisable family members.

4

+1 · Sandhi — Sound combination rules

Now you know धृतराष्ट्रः should have a ः. So where did it go? Visarga Lopa — Pāṇini 8.3.17. The ः disappears before उ. One rule, every speaker, every chapter.

Sandhi makes no sense until you know what the original form should be. That is why Sandhi comes last — not first.

The Root

One dhātu. Nine encounters across the Gita.

The student starts with उवाच and — by the end of the course — recognises all these forms as members of the same family. This is the dhātu-first approach: one root, multiple contexts, progressive recognition.
